DSCA Signal-to-Decision Flow

How Two Engines Become One Curriculum Decision · Per Scholas Curriculum Solutions
🎯

The destination: from reactive to proactive

Per Scholas curriculum should not be built in response to requests we receive. It should be built from blueprints we have ready — informed by employer signal and internal feedback — so we deliver on what the market needs before stakeholders formally ask. DSCA is the mechanism that makes that shift possible.

The two engines
Engine 1
External Market Signal
Signal from employer advisors. What does the market need? Which roles are hiring? Which certifications matter? Which skills are outdated?
  • Focus groups (Macro / Micro)
  • À la carte advisor conversations
  • Pre-session surveys
  • JTA validation sessions
Engine 2
Internal Feedback Loop
Signal from inside Per Scholas. What is working in delivery? What is broken? Which courses produce strong outcomes? Where do learners struggle?
  • Instructor observations (IDQA)
  • Manager LEAD performance data
  • Canvas help-desk intake
  • Instructor NPS
🧩

Engine 2, refined: four feedback audiences, two signal types

Discovery with Shahparan Hasnat (VP, Product Development & Operations, Jun 16) sharpened how internal feedback is structured. Engine 2 is not one audience — it is up to four, and each gives a different type of signal. The architecture recommendation (mid-July) will reflect this.

1 · Employer partnersExternal validation — already covered by Engine 1 / DSCA today.
2 · InstructorsDelivery-side signal — what works and breaks in the live classroom.
3 · Curriculum developersDesign-side signal — sequencing, scope, structure of the curriculum itself.
4 · Employed graduatesClosed-loop signal — alumni working in the domain become quasi-employer feedback.

The design-vs-delivery distinction. Two signals look alike but are not. Design feedback is about the recipe — is the curriculum sequenced and scoped correctly? Delivery feedback is about the cooking — did this instance run well? The same course can be well-designed but poorly delivered, or vice versa. Engine 2 must separate these so a delivery hiccup is not mistaken for a design flaw.

How one signal moves from input to decision

1A · Engine 1 input arrives

A Product Owner runs a focus group, completes an à la carte conversation, or receives survey data. The PO synthesizes findings.

Source: Employer advisor

1B · Engine 2 input arrives

An instructor reports an outdated lab, LEAD identifies a domain-level outcome gap, or a Canvas ticket flags a delivery issue.

Source: Internal team
▼

2A · Logged in DSCA base

PO enters synthesized findings via the À La Carte Form or Focus Group Form. The signal lands in the DSCA Airtable base — the single source of truth.

Airtable · DSCA base

2B · Logged in IFF Session Log

Internal signal enters through the only legitimate intake channel (Canvas form). Per Scholas feedback taxonomy is applied at intake — categorized by severity.

Canvas → Airtable
▼

3 · Triage at the weekly Design Meeting

Both engines' signals are reviewed in one room. Sr. Manager of Product Operations convenes; Sr. Director of Product Management presides. Each signal gets a decision within 48 hours: Accept (will act on this), Defer (valid but not now), or Decline (not actionable). System-broken signals are triaged the same week; design-content signals follow a longer cadence aligned to refresh cycles.

Weekly Design Meeting · Asana decision log
▼

4 · Pattern detection

A single signal from one cohort is logged. A pattern of the same signal across two or more cohorts — or the same signal showing up in both Engine 1 and Engine 2 — is the strongest trigger. The Synthesis Dashboard in Airtable rolls up all input live, filterable by product, so patterns are visible at a glance.

Airtable Synthesis Dashboard
▼

5 · Phase 0 Refresh Intake

When a pattern is confirmed, it opens a Phase 0 refresh request. Manager LEAD validates the signal and packages the evidence. Sr. Director of Product Management authorizes the refresh. The relevant Director, PO takes it into the SOP pipeline (design → build → launch).

SOP Phase 0 → Phase 2
▼

6 · Close the loop

Once a recommendation is locked, it is shared with Enterprise Solutions (Laura + Nadira). They close the loop with the advisors who participated — confirming that their input was heard and where it landed. The cycle starts again with the next product on the roadmap.

Enterprise Solutions · "What We Heard" summary
Two principles guiding how we operate
Principle 1 · Speed

Signal that arrives late is signal that arrives wrong

The markets we serve move at AI speed. If a partner tells us in March that machine learning is fading and we react in October, the signal was true but the response is useless. Capturing a signal is the easy part. The hard part is the action that follows when the signal arrives — and the latency from signal in to decision out is itself a measurable thing we should not be afraid to look at.

"In AI-speed markets, a slow signal system is worse than no signal system."
Principle 2 · Adoption

A system on paper that nobody follows is not a system

We have SOPs. We have playbooks. We have tools. None of that matters if the people who are supposed to use them do not. DSCA must be designed so that the people contributing to it — Product Owners, instructors, IDQA, Enterprise Solutions — see it as a tool that helps them, not as another task imposed on top of their work. Adoption is the proof. Documentation is not.

"SOPs on paper that nobody follows are not SOPs."

🔗

The strongest trigger: when both engines say the same thing

The two engines run independently — different sources, different SLAs, different triage paths. But they converge at Airtable and at the triage committee. When external market signal (Engine 1) and internal delivery feedback (Engine 2) point to the same gap, that is the strongest possible trigger for a curriculum decision. It means both the employer ecosystem and our own delivery system agree something must change. That convergence is what DSCA exists to surface.

DSCA Evidence Trace

Closing the loop: advisor signal → curriculum decision → licensing citation
Per Scholas | Curriculum Solutions Owner: Viktor Shumylo, Director, Product Strategy & Programs
Built with: Aishwarya Bhave, Sr. Director, Product Management
Status: v1 · Jun 24

Why this exists

The Employer Curriculum Advisory Boards charter sets a product-specific KPI: 70% of curriculum licensing submissions should cite advisor findings as part of the justification. Today there is strong advisor recruitment and engagement — but no systematic trace from a specific piece of advisor feedback to the curriculum decision it shaped to the licensing submission that cites it.

The Evidence Trace closes that gap. For every market-driven refresh, three columns are captured inside the design team's existing evidence worksheet — so the trace is a byproduct of the work, not a separate task layered on top.

✓ Approved with Bolaji Saibu in the Product Design meeting, Jun 23 — folded into the curriculum refresh template
1

What advisors said

The raw signal. Captured from focus groups, à la carte conversations, surveys, or JTA sessions and synthesized by the Product Owner.

e.g. "Cyber advisors across two focus groups flagged that SOC analysts now need hands-on SIEM tuning, not just theory."
→
2

What we changed or validated

The curriculum decision that resulted. Either a change made to scope/sequence/content, or an explicit validation that the current design already meets the need.

e.g. "Added a SIEM tuning lab to Module 4; expanded hands-on hours from 6 to 10."
→
3

Where it shows up

The proof point. The specific outline section, JTA task, or roadmap item where the change is now reflected — and, where applicable, the licensing submission that cites it.

e.g. "UCI 1033 outline §4.2; JTA task 4.2.1; cited in June licensing submission."

The live Evidence Trace

The trace lives inside the design team's market-driven refresh evidence worksheet, so advisor input, the resulting decision, and the proof point are all captured in one place as the refresh work happens. Links open the working artifacts directly.
